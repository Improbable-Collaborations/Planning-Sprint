# Hitchhiker's Guide to Earth: An Invitation to Collaborate

*A conversation starter on story, culture, and constitutional inquiry — Spring 2026*

## Background

We start with a simple observation: some stories shape how large numbers of people think about the world, power, and how decisions are made together — often more effectively than formal theories or institutions.

One such story is *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* franchise by Douglas Adams.

First published in the late 1970s, the Hitchhiker books are widely known as comic science fiction. Beneath the humour, the work explores serious themes: complexity, bureaucracy, overconfidence, technology, and the limits of human understanding. Douglas Adams was deeply interested in science, evolution, and the unintended consequences of human systems.

The books have sold over 15 million copies worldwide and have become part of shared cultural literacy. Millions of copies were sold in 30 languages — one of which is Catalan. Many people aged 45 and over know the work well, and its ideas, phrases, and characters continue to circulate across generations. This is not a niche reference, but a widely recognised cultural framework with long-standing influence and a committed following.

Our team, along with Robbie Stamp — Douglas Adams' long-time collaborator and co-founder of h2g2.com — hold the rights to the intellectual property of the books for use in real-world guidance. Our mission is to honour that inheritance by putting it to work where it matters most: helping people across cultures, disciplines, and generations engage meaningfully with the governance questions that will shape how we live together on this planet. We believe this is exactly what Douglas would have wanted — not a museum piece, but a living framework for collective sense-making at a moment when it has never been more needed.

## The Question

At its core, the current Hitchhiker's project asks a question that feels increasingly relevant today:

> *How do humans make decisions together in systems that are far larger, faster, and more complex than their ability to fully understand?*

This is a constitutional question, but it is not only a legal one. It sits at the intersection of law, governance, science, technology, culture, and public participation. Answering it seriously requires methods that are interdisciplinary by design, experimentally grounded, and accessible to the public — not only to specialists.

We are building a year-long participatory constitutional drafting process that draws on exactly these methods. **This document is an invitation to explore whether your institution might be interested in joining that work.**

## Why Hitchhiker's — and Why It Matters

Consider what Adams actually wrote about. Earth is demolished to make way for an interstellar bypass — a decision made by a remote bureaucracy so vast that no citizen could meaningfully participate in it, announced through channels no one could reasonably be expected to monitor. The plans were "on display" in a locked filing cabinet, in a disused lavatory, with a sign on the door saying *Beware of the Leopard*. This is not just comedy. It is a working description of how democratic legitimacy fails when institutions scale beyond the capacity of the people they govern.

The story's central computer, Deep Thought, spends millions of years calculating the Answer to the Ultimate Question — and produces "42." The problem was never the answer. The problem was that no one understood what question they were actually asking. This is the predicament of technocratic governance: systems that optimise brilliantly for the wrong objectives, because the process of defining the question was never opened to genuine public deliberation.

The Vogons — administrators who destroy planets while filling in the correct paperwork — are not alien. They are a portrait of procedural authority detached from human consequence. The Babel fish, which translates all languages perfectly, causes more wars than anything else in history — because removing barriers to communication does not, by itself, produce understanding. These are not imaginative flights. They are constitutional problems, rendered in a language that millions of readers already share.

Throughout the story, Adams consistently challenges blind faith in bureaucracy, technocratic certainty, and the assumption that order, measurement, and procedure automatically produce wisdom. Instead, the work points toward humility, adaptability, humour, and shared sense-making as necessary responses to uncertainty.

The intention here is not to treat Hitchhiker's as a solution or a model for governance. Rather, it can function as a shared reference point that helps people talk about complexity without panic, question authority without cynicism, and think together across disciplines, cultures, and generations. Because the story is already widely known and emotionally resonant, it lowers the barrier to engagement and opens space for serious inquiry — particularly with students who may feel alienated by abstract or overly technical approaches to governance.

That is why we use it: not to make the work less serious, but to make serious work more accessible.

## What the Project Does

The project convenes scholars, governance practitioners, technologists, students, and citizens around a shared inquiry: what would a constitutional framework adequate to the twenty-first century actually require?

Rather than producing a single theoretical text, the process is structured as interconnected workshops, assemblies, and collaborative research exercises — each surfacing different dimensions of the question.

- **Comparative constitutional analysis** — examining how societies have responded to institutional transition, drawing on case studies from post-conflict constitution-writing, climate governance, and emerging legal domains such as space law.
- **Citizen assemblies and deliberative workshops** — structured processes through which non-specialists engage with governance questions, contribute local knowledge, and test proposed constitutional language against lived experience.
- **Science fiction as speculative method** — using narrative, backcasting, and scenario-building as rigorous tools for surfacing assumptions, exploring consequences, and making abstract governance concepts tangible.
- **Computational and socio-technical governance** — exploring how constitutional values might be expressed through digital systems, trust networks, privacy-preserving infrastructure, and participatory platforms.
- **Legislative theatre and performative inquiry** — methods drawn from Augusto Boal's tradition, adapted for governance contexts, in which participants rehearse institutional scenarios through embodied deliberation.

Each strand feeds into the others. The constitutional text is iteratively shaped by legal scholarship, public deliberation, technical analysis, and creative inquiry working in parallel. The aim is convergence through method, not through premature consensus.

## Why This Matters for a University

**Research impact and interdisciplinary innovation.** The project sits at a productive intersection of constitutional law, governance studies, STS, futures research, participatory design, and digital humanities. It creates opportunities for collaborative publications, cross-departmental research, and grant applications aligned with European frameworks that prize trans-disciplinary approaches.

**Student engagement with real-world governance.** Students do not simply study governance — they practise it, through workshop facilitation, legal drafting exercises, comparative research, field notes, media production, or technical prototyping. Contributions are substantive, credited, and integrated into outputs.

**Civic relevance in Catalonia and beyond.** Catalonia has its own deep relationship with constitutional questions — sovereignty, linguistic rights, institutional design, democratic legitimacy. This project offers a framework within which Catalan scholars and students can bring their expertise to a broader international conversation, with collaborators already working across multiple European countries and the UK.

## Reciprocal Working Agreement for Professors and Students

One of the distinguishing features of the Hitchhiker's Guide to Earth project is its commitment to federated, non-hierarchical collaboration. This shapes how we think about student involvement.

Rather than offering one-way placement or observation opportunities, we are interested in building a genuine exchange — where professors and students bring their expertise, perspectives, and curiosity, and receive something meaningful in return.

**What we offer:**

- Active participation in a live, internationally networked cultural and governance project
- Mentorship and collaborative working relationships with practitioners across governance, technology, cultural production, and community organising
- Co-authorship credit on any outputs where contribution is substantive
- Access to the H2GE network of community catalysts across multiple countries and cultural contexts
- The opportunity to contribute to, and be named in, any funding applications or institutional partnerships that develop from shared work
- A reference letter or formal recognition of contribution, tailored to any student's own professional or academic development goals

**What we ask:**

- Genuine intellectual engagement with the project's questions — not just task completion
- Willingness to work across disciplines and in formats that may be unfamiliar
- A commitment to reciprocity: sharing what you learn, and being open about what you don't know
- A minimum engagement period of one academic term, with regular check-ins to ensure the relationship remains useful for both parties

Any professor, class, or student wishing to collaborate formally would be invited to co-author a brief working agreement at the outset — covering scope, expected outputs, time commitment, and attribution. This is not a contract, but a shared document of intent, revisable by either party. It reflects the project's broader philosophy: that good collaboration begins with honest conversation about what each person needs and can offer.

We are open to students working at any level — undergraduate, postgraduate, or doctoral — and across any relevant discipline, including governance, cultural studies, law, technology, design, anthropology, and communications.
