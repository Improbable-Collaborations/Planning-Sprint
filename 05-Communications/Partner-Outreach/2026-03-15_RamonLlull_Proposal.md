# An Invitation to Collaborate on Participatory Constitutional Inquiry

*A proposal for academic partnership — Spring 2026*

## The Question

How do societies write the rules they live by when the systems those rules must govern — climate, technology, migration, digital identity, ecological collapse — move faster than any single discipline can follow?

This is a constitutional question, but it is not only a legal one. It sits at the intersection of law, governance, science, technology, culture, and public participation. Answering it seriously requires methods that are interdisciplinary by design, experimentally grounded, and accessible to the public — not only to specialists.

We are building a year-long participatory constitutional drafting process that draws on exactly these methods. This note is an invitation to explore whether your institution might be interested in joining that work.

## What the Project Does

The project convenes scholars, governance practitioners, technologists, students, and citizens around a shared inquiry: what would a constitutional framework adequate to the twenty-first century actually require?

Rather than producing a single theoretical text, the process is structured as a series of interconnected workshops, assemblies, and collaborative research exercises — each designed to surface different dimensions of the question.

The methodology is deliberately pluralist:

- **Comparative constitutional analysis** — examining how different societies have responded to moments of institutional transition, drawing on case studies from post-conflict constitution-writing, climate governance, and emerging legal domains such as space law.
- **Citizen assemblies and deliberative workshops** — structured processes through which non-specialists engage with governance questions, contribute local knowledge, and test proposed constitutional language against lived experience.
- **Science fiction as speculative method** — using narrative, backcasting, and imaginative scenario-building not as decoration but as a rigorous tool for surfacing assumptions, exploring consequences, and making abstract governance concepts tangible. This draws on a growing body of academic work that treats speculative fiction as a legitimate mode of futures research and policy design.
- **Computational and socio-technical governance** — exploring how constitutional values might be expressed through digital systems, trust networks, privacy-preserving infrastructure, and participatory platforms — and how those systems can remain democratically accountable.
- **Legislative theatre and performative inquiry** — methods drawn from Augusto Boal's tradition, adapted for governance contexts, in which participants rehearse institutional scenarios and test constitutional propositions through embodied deliberation.

Each workshop strand feeds into the others. The constitutional text being drafted is iteratively shaped by legal scholarship, public deliberation, technical analysis, and creative inquiry working in parallel. The aim is convergence through method, not through premature consensus.

## Why This Matters for a University

Universities are uniquely positioned to contribute to this kind of work — and to benefit from it.

**Research impact and interdisciplinary innovation.** The project sits at a genuinely productive intersection of constitutional law, governance studies, STS (science and technology studies), futures research, participatory design, and digital humanities. It creates opportunities for collaborative publications, cross-departmental research initiatives, and grant applications aligned with European frameworks that increasingly prize transdisciplinary approaches to societal challenges.

**Student engagement with real-world governance.** The participatory structure of the project means students do not simply study governance — they practise it. Students at any level can contribute through workshop facilitation, legal drafting exercises, comparative research, field notes, media production, or technical prototyping. Their contributions are substantive, credited, and integrated into the project's outputs.

**Civic relevance in Catalonia and beyond.** Catalonia has its own deep relationship with constitutional questions — questions of sovereignty, linguistic rights, institutional design, and democratic legitimacy. This project does not presume to address those questions directly, but it offers a framework within which Catalan scholars and students can bring their particular expertise and perspective to a broader, international conversation. The project already has collaborators across multiple European countries, the UK, and beyond.

**The role of science fiction and speculative methods.** The use of science fiction as a research and engagement tool is increasingly recognised in academic contexts — from MIT's Science Fiction to Science Fabrication programme to the European Commission's use of speculative design in foresight exercises. This project applies those methods specifically to constitutional and governance inquiry, which remains relatively unexplored territory and a genuine opportunity for original scholarly contribution.

## How the Process Works

The year-long programme is organised in phases:

1. **Convening and scoping** — identifying participating institutions, defining shared research questions, establishing working agreements.
2. **Workshop series** — a distributed programme of workshops across partner institutions, each focusing on a different methodological strand (comparative law, citizen assembly, speculative fiction, computational governance, legislative theatre). Workshops are designed to produce both scholarly outputs and contributions to the constitutional drafting process.
3. **Synthesis and drafting** — integrating findings across workshop strands into an evolving constitutional text, tested through further deliberation.
4. **Publication and dissemination** — academic papers, public-facing materials, and a final constitutional text presented as both a scholarly and civic contribution.

The process is governed by the participants themselves. The constitution being drafted is also intended to govern the project — meaning questions of ownership, contribution, decision-making, and resource-sharing are not abstract principles but working realities that the group must negotiate together. This reflexive dimension is itself a research contribution.

## Who Is Involved

The project is led by the Hitchhiker Research Fellows, a network of practitioners and scholars working across governance innovation, computational law, participatory democracy, digital systems, and cultural production. The constitutional scholarship informing the process draws significantly on the work of Dr Heather Allansdottir, whose research in comparative constitutional law, climate governance, and space law provides a rigorous legal foundation for the inquiry.

Collaborators bring experience in citizen assembly design, trust network architecture, privacy-preserving systems, legislative theatre, ecological jurisprudence, and corporate governance — spanning the range of expertise needed for constitutional thinking at this scale.

## What We Are Looking For

We are seeking academic partners who are interested in one or more of the following:

- Co-hosting or contributing to a workshop within the year-long programme — whether focused on constitutional law, governance, STS, futures studies, or public engagement.
- Involving students (undergraduate through doctoral) in substantive research or facilitation roles, with full attribution and co-authorship where appropriate.
- Exploring joint funding applications, particularly within European research frameworks.
- Contributing disciplinary expertise — legal, technical, cultural, ecological — to the constitutional drafting process.

Collaboration is structured through a brief mutual working agreement covering scope, outputs, time commitment, and attribution — not a rigid contract, but a shared document of intent, revisable by either party.

## Next Steps

This note is a starting point. If it resonates, we would welcome a conversation about how your department, research group, or students might engage — and what form of collaboration would be most useful from your perspective.

The ambition is straightforward: constitutional thinking for the twenty-first century needs to be more participatory, more interdisciplinary, more technologically literate, and more publicly accessible than the forms we have inherited. We are trying to build a process that takes that seriously.

We would be glad to discuss further.
