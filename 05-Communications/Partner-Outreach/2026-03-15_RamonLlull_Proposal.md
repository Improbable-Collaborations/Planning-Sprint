# An Invitation to Collaborate on Participatory Constitutional Inquiry

*A proposal for academic partnership — Spring 2026*

## The Question

How do societies write the rules they live by when the systems those rules must govern — climate, technology, migration, digital identity, ecological collapse — move faster than any single discipline can follow?

This is a constitutional question, but it is not only a legal one. It sits at the intersection of law, governance, science, technology, culture, and public participation. Answering it seriously requires methods that are interdisciplinary by design, experimentally grounded, and accessible to the public — not only to specialists.

We are building a year-long participatory constitutional drafting process that draws on exactly these methods. This note is an invitation to explore whether your institution might be interested in joining that work.

## Hitchhikers Guide and the role of Science Fiction

The use of speculative fiction as a research tool is increasingly recognised in academic contexts — from MIT's Science Fiction to Science Fabrication programme to the European Commission's use of speculative design in foresight exercises. Narrative makes abstract governance questions tangible. It surfaces assumptions. It lets people rehearse futures before committing to them.

This project uses *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* as its public framework — not as branding, but because the story is an uncannily precise diagnosis of the governance crisis we face now.

Consider what Adams actually wrote about. Earth is demolished to make way for an interstellar bypass — a decision made by a remote bureaucracy so vast that no citizen could meaningfully participate in it, announced through channels no one could reasonably be expected to monitor. The plans were "on display" in a locked filing cabinet, in a disused lavatory, with a sign on the door saying *Beware of the Leopard*. This is not just comedy. It is a working description of how democratic legitimacy fails when institutions scale beyond the capacity of the people they govern.

The story's central computer, Deep Thought, spends millions of years calculating the Answer to the Ultimate Question — and produces "42." The problem was never the answer. The problem was that no one understood what question they were actually asking. This is the predicament of technocratic governance: systems that optimise brilliantly for the wrong objectives, because the process of defining the question was never opened to genuine public deliberation.

The Vogons — administrators who destroy planets while filling in the correct paperwork — are not alien. They are a portrait of procedural authority detached from human consequence. The Babel fish, which translates all languages perfectly, causes more wars than anything else in history — because removing barriers to communication does not, by itself, produce understanding. These are not imaginative flights. They are constitutional problems, rendered in a language that 15 million readers already share.

Adams was deeply interested in science, evolution, and the unintended consequences of complex systems. His work — available in 30 languages including Catalan — functions as a shared cultural vocabulary through which people across disciplines, generations, and cultures can engage with governance questions without the alienation that purely technical or legal language often produces. That is why we use it: not to make the work less serious, but to make serious work more accessible.

## What the Project Does

The project convenes scholars, governance practitioners, technologists, students, and citizens around a shared inquiry: what would a constitutional framework adequate to the twenty-first century actually require?

Rather than producing a single theoretical text, the process is structured as interconnected workshops, assemblies, and collaborative research exercises — each surfacing different dimensions of the question.

- **Comparative constitutional analysis** — examining how societies have responded to institutional transition, drawing on case studies from post-conflict constitution-writing, climate governance, and emerging legal domains such as space law.
- **Citizen assemblies and deliberative workshops** — structured processes through which non-specialists engage with governance questions, contribute local knowledge, and test proposed constitutional language against lived experience.
- **Science fiction as speculative method** — using narrative, backcasting, and scenario-building as rigorous tools for surfacing assumptions, exploring consequences, and making abstract governance concepts tangible.
- **Computational and socio-technical governance** — exploring how constitutional values might be expressed through digital systems, trust networks, privacy-preserving infrastructure, and participatory platforms.
- **Legislative theatre and performative inquiry** — methods drawn from Augusto Boal's tradition, adapted for governance contexts, in which participants rehearse institutional scenarios through embodied deliberation.

Each strand feeds into the others. The constitutional text is iteratively shaped by legal scholarship, public deliberation, technical analysis, and creative inquiry working in parallel. The aim is convergence through method, not through premature consensus.

## Why This Matters for a University

**Research impact and interdisciplinary innovation.** The project sits at a productive intersection of constitutional law, governance studies, STS, futures research, participatory design, and digital humanities. It creates opportunities for collaborative publications, cross-departmental research, and grant applications aligned with European frameworks that prize transdisciplinary approaches.

**Student engagement with real-world governance.** Students do not simply study governance — they practise it, through workshop facilitation, legal drafting exercises, comparative research, field notes, media production, or technical prototyping. Contributions are substantive, credited, and integrated into outputs.

**Civic relevance in Catalonia and beyond.** Catalonia has its own deep relationship with constitutional questions — sovereignty, linguistic rights, institutional design, democratic legitimacy. This project offers a framework within which Catalan scholars and students can bring their expertise to a broader international conversation, with collaborators already working across multiple European countries and the UK.

## How the Process Works

1. **Convening and scoping** — identifying participating institutions, defining shared research questions, establishing working agreements.
2. **Workshop series** — a distributed programme across partner institutions, each focusing on a different methodological strand. Workshops produce both scholarly outputs and contributions to the constitutional drafting process.
3. **Synthesis and drafting** — integrating findings into an evolving constitutional text, tested through further deliberation.
4. **Publication and dissemination** — academic papers, public-facing materials, and a final constitutional text presented as both a scholarly and civic contribution.

The constitution being drafted is also intended to govern the project itself — meaning questions of ownership, contribution, decision-making, and resource-sharing are working realities the group negotiates together. This reflexive dimension is itself a research contribution.

## What We Are Looking For

We are seeking academic partners interested in:

- Co-hosting or contributing to a workshop — whether focused on constitutional law, governance, STS, futures studies, or public engagement.
- Involving students (undergraduate through doctoral) in substantive research or facilitation roles, with full attribution and co-authorship where appropriate.
- Exploring joint funding applications, particularly within European research frameworks.
- Contributing disciplinary expertise — legal, technical, cultural, ecological — to the constitutional drafting process.

Collaboration is structured through a brief mutual working agreement covering scope, outputs, time commitment, and attribution — a shared document of intent, revisable by either party.

## Next Steps

This note is a starting point. If it resonates, the next step would be a conversation about how your department, research group, or students might engage — and what form of collaboration would be most useful from your perspective.

The ambition is straightforward: constitutional thinking for the twenty-first century needs to be more participatory, more interdisciplinary, more technologically literate, and more publicly accessible than the forms we have inherited. We are trying to build a process that takes that seriously.
