## Basics of the Pan-Galactic Time Bank

**David Bovill:**  
So it would be the world's first global time bank. There have been a couple of kind of time-bankish projects—I think one might be called Improbable by some supermodel, I've forgotten her name, English supermodel—which was effectively a time bank but a kind of playful creative currency mobile app. Jimmy Wales supported it, something like that. But really there is no such thing, and there's never been an attempt to do a global time bank.

All time banks are generally structured and encouraged to be **hyper-local** time banks in a particular community. Most of the skills offered are things like classically massage, or picking up shopping for an old person, or community development work. There are some time banks which are business-to-business and so on. I know that world really well; I spent a good 10 years in it. I was there right at the beginning. I met Edgar Cahn. I know the community. I know the issues, how it relates to other forms of community currency.

There'll be somewhat of a pushback, because the general ethos of classical time banks is that it's designed for real community work at the local level. The idea that it would be used for young people working, coding things, and playing computer games doesn't usually gel—which is one of the reasons they haven't applied it in that context. But that's basically what we'll be doing: a **global time bank**, particularly around the kind of **digital work** we need:

- Working on **guides**
- Researching a community or field researchers for a particular guide
- Audio editing on the podcast for that guide
- Taking audio submissions from interviews and editing them, or giving them to an LLM and processing them
- Graphic design or illustrating the guide
- **Coding**, or writing specs and vibe-coding
- **Learning and teaching** (Academy): e.g. teaching how to onboard, how to vibe-code UIs and do agentic coding—you can pay for those with time credits and earn it back by passing the knowledge on

So unlike most time banks where you run out of people and skills locally, when we go **global** and focus on what we actually need people to do, it'll work much better than normal time banks.

## How It Works: Pledges, Overdraft, and Projects

**David Bovill:**  
The way we do it is an extreme version of time bank practice.

1. **Community events** – Like the annual galactic meeting. Everyone who wants a ticket or to join can get it by signing up and making a pledge, then they get invites to interesting events. That's part of the marketing to get people to sign up.

2. **Storytelling and ceremony** – The main marketing is the overall storytelling about this creative/weird alternative pan-galactic economy. The act of joining has a **ceremony where you pledge**. When you pledge **42 hours** (describing your interests, skills, and what you want to learn or explore in the universe), that onboarding is the sign-up for the time bank.

3. **42 hours = credit/overdraft** – When you pledge 42 hours, you get 42 hours of **positive credit**—effectively your overdraft that you repay over the year. It's backed by the pledge. The pledge is most probably **audio or video**, deposited in the **pledge bank**. We have the domain pledgebank.org / pledgebank.earth—a site where these videos are held in the vault. Like a mortgage backing a loan: we deposit the pledge, and in return the bank can create currency in your account that you're pledging to redeem/pay back over the year.

4. **More currency** – Want more? Pledge more. Want to crowdfund? Get your community to pledge more. When you pledge and get credit, you can donate or invest that currency in a **community project** (e.g. a guide). E.g. 42 credits → give 20 to Hitchhiker's Guide to Democracy, 10 to another project, keep 10. Or set it so e.g. 42% automatically goes to community things, randomly or by direction.

5. **Projects = guides** – Projects have their own bank accounts with time credits. The community makes personal pledges and invests in a particular guide. E.g. Hitchhiker's Guide to Democracy is crowdfunded and has 10,000 hours in its account. The project manager (the Hitchhiker) can spend that—often back to people who pledged, but not necessarily. Mutual credit / time bank: overall balance should tend to **zero**; it's not a speculative currency.

## Dynamic Equity and Guides

**David Bovill:**  
With classic "slicing the pie" **dynamic equity**, you need infrastructure to account for time and invoicing by all team members, and it helps to know what skills they have. In a larger community (e.g. 150 people on a guide), knowing skills and matching—e.g. "we need graphic design, six hours, register your work"—means you're running as a **deep organization / dynamic equity partnership**. You don't award time credits; you translate **monitored time** into **commercial value** reflected in a **changed percentage of equity** in the project.

You can run a **time bank project** and then **pivot**: "We've been running this for fun with time credits; we're now offering to turn it into something that generates real revenue. We'll create a revenue-sharing guide—as people buy it, you get paid by your dynamic equity." E.g. "Max, you've got 200 hours of time credits from work on this project; you can invest them in the guide's dynamic equity partnership and now have 80% of the equity. People who keep using time credits just as time credits don't get dynamic equity."

**Max G:**  
So the idea is guides can become for-profit and switch to generating money—how does that relate?

**David Bovill:**  
Ideally as many as possible generate money; they go for sale. There's a balance of people **buying** things and people **contributing time**; the two work together.

**David Bovill (constitution / IP):**  
Default is probably three options when creating a guide (to be decided in the Constitution):

1. **Commons / public good** – Doing it for the public good, put it in the Hitchhiker commons. Any equity/revenue funds Hitchhiker infrastructure, core work, R&D, commons/foundation. Maybe sponsors an extra Hitchhiker or two for a 12-month thing. You might target where money goes—e.g. a guide on intergenerational work or loneliness. We're working on this guide because we like it; any money goes to the foundation or community.

2. **Fully private / spin-off** – "I want to make money; spin it off as a private company." E.g. Hitchhiker's Guide to India as the guide for everyone going to India, then closing, mobile app, hotel chain, etc. As long as you don't use Hitchhiker IP/characters, you can take the project and commercialize it. We encourage that; we have software and partners. We'd like credit for incubating; if you become rich, a donation would be nice.

3. **Middle: tithing** – You can make money and use some of our IP/Earth characters, but we want e.g. a **4.2% tithe** on revenue because we incubated you. Different levels of support for the three: lots for community ones, more business support for tithing ones, maybe less for simple private spin-offs. We might start with only foundation ones and add the others later.

**David Bovill (legal separation of time bank and equity):**  
We don't want to **contaminate** the time bank with commercial value or equity. Best to **separate** them and use the same underlying codebase. Legally: run it as a time bank with **no promise** of value, no tradable thing, no offer of shares. Then later the **community** can decide: "Would you like to start a commercial dynamic equity partnership?" If yes, we **may** offer starting dynamic equity shares based on previous balances—as long as there's **no guarantee or logical tie**, just an option (like saying "you get 40%, you get 20%" when starting a project), it should be legally defensible.

There are advantages: progressive commitment—start with time bank, decide later when to get more serious; you're not trapped from day one. If we don't allow that, then once you're in the time bank you can't easily switch later. Downside: when we do dynamic equity we go against the classical time bank ethos; the global time bank network might say "it's not really a time bank—you can get shares and make money." So we have to balance, talk to people, and decide who we're okay to piss off; you can't please everyone.

## Technical Strategy: Radically Simple, Local-First, Web2 Ceremony / Web3

**David Bovill:**  
I have previous recordings and pages on this; Claude can run over them. The last bit is the **technical strategy** I'd like: **radically simple**, vibe-coded, **local-first**, and **Web2 ceremony / Web3**.

- A simple time bank only needs a **tiny text file** on your phone: last month or three months, last hundred or so transactions with other community members you trust. It's not commercially sensitive; the ethos is that people don't cheat; you're meeting everyone in your group anyway. You register "I did four hours with you, Max, you said thank you" and add it. You do it with people you have a relationship with; you're just keeping a balance. The usual danger isn't theft—it's people doing things for free and not bothering to register because of bureaucracy and embarrassment.

**Max G:**  
So just having a text record in a mobile app that's convenient is kind of all you need.

**David Bovill:**  
And you can hyperlink to the wiki page, hear the audio of the report and the thank you. Because it's so small and not reliant on a database—just text file, JSON, a bit of JavaScript—you can implement it in many languages and keep it simple.

**Clearing ceremony** – Periodically (e.g. once a week) you press a button and **check**: your record of "you paid me on these dates" should match the other person's ledger (mirror image). In a **review meeting**, everyone sends their text/ledger; the system produces feedback—how many hours the whole group did, charts—and **flags** any mismatches or corruption into a "to be sorted out" list. The project manager says "that was great, here's feedback," then presses a button. That is **digitally signed** and **stored on a ledger** as the record of the community's involvement.

So: **lightweight, Web2-style messaging** between devices, minimal surface (you could edit the JSON); then a **clearing ceremony** where we "burn" the agreed state to a durable record. That allows a lot of vibe-coding agility in the front end and how it ties to wikis, Markdown, Obsidian; you can add digital signatures later. When you need **Web3**—e.g. dynamic equity or long-term historical records (e.g. "what Southeast England did")—you take these **regular snapshots** with human-in-the-loop agreement and store them robustly.
