# Process Work / Process-Oriented Psychology

**Arnold Mindell, 1980s onward**

---

Mindell started as a Jungian analyst and physicist, developing a method that works with conflict, power, and rank as dynamic signals rather than problems to be managed. "World Work" is the large-group application — participants occupy roles in a living field, rank is surfaced and worked with consciously, edges (internal resistance to change) are followed rather than bypassed. Deep democracy is the operating principle: every voice, including the disturbing and marginalised one, carries information the system needs.

## Strengths

- Uniquely equipped to handle high-conflict, high-charge situations
- The rank framework (social, psychological, spiritual, contextual) is extraordinarily useful
- Doesn't pretend conflict can be designed away
- Has been used in post-conflict settings (South Africa, Middle East)

## Weaknesses

- Requires highly skilled facilitators — not a method you pick up in a weekend
- Can destabilise groups that aren't ready for depth
- The therapeutic origins can make organisational clients uncomfortable
- Risk of facilitator projection

## Eastern Fit

Moderate. The Taoist underpinnings (following the river, not fighting it) resonate in East Asian contexts. The psychological directness around rank and power can clash in high-context, face-preserving cultures (Japan, parts of SE Asia, hierarchical India). Mindell himself has worked in Japan, with interesting adaptations.

## H2GE Relevance

Medium. The rank framework is essential for an organisation working across cultures, classes, and power asymmetries. World Work could inform Demolition Day events — surfacing what needs to be destroyed. The deep democracy principle aligns with constitutional work.
